This week we got the design brief from BlaBla Corp. The task is to build a community hub where people with a shared interest can come together, share things that matter to them, and keep coming back. The brief gives a lot of creative freedom, which is exciting, but that also means the hardest part is picking the right idea.

My first instinct was to think about communities I am actually part of or genuinely care about, rather than picking something that sounds good on paper. I came up with three directions.

The first was a financial literacy hub for women. A space where people can share tips, resources, and personal experiences around investing and money, without the intimidating tone that a lot of finance content has. The challenge I could already see was scope, since finance is a big and sensitive topic, and getting the community features right without it becoming overwhelming felt risky.

The second was a food community focused on deals and discount apps. I liked the practical utility but wasn't convinced it was original enough. The space feels saturated and I couldn't see what would make this hub different from just a group chat.

The third was a design community hub for sharing tools and resources. It felt too generic. I couldn't land on a core feature that would make it something you'd actually come back to.

After sharing our individual ideas, the team landed on Pat's idea, BrewHub, a community for coffee and matcha enthusiasts where people could log daily brews, share tasting notes, and rate recipes. The design challenge was genuinely interesting but after our tutorial discussion I started to have doubts. When I thought about it honestly, it felt too close to what BlaBla Corp was already giving us: a discussion board with a coffee aesthetic. The brief asked for something that genuinely served the community in a way that felt new, and BrewHub didn't clear that bar.

So we chose to pivot.

![Decision-making flowchart](assets/post2-decision-flow.svg)

Once we committed to that, we set up a GitHub Projects board to track what we needed to build. We'd been loosely keeping tasks in a shared doc but it wasn't working. GitHub Projects let us assign issues, mark things as in progress, and see at a glance what was blocked or waiting on someone else. It made it much easier to stop duplicating effort, especially once we started building in parallel.

The key question we started asking ourselves was: what does this community actually need that they can't already get? That question led us somewhere much more personal.
