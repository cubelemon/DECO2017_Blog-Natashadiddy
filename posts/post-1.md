This week we got the design brief from BlaBla Corp. The task is to build a community hub, a space where people with a shared interest can come together, share things that matter to them, and keep coming back. The brief gives us a lot of creative freedom which is exciting but also means the hardest part is picking the right idea.

My first instinct was to think about communities I'm actually part of or genuinely care about, rather than just picking something that sounds good on paper. I came up with three directions.

The first was a financial literacy hub for women. A space where people can share tips, resources, and personal experiences around investing and money, without the intimidating tone that a lot of finance content has. I was most personally drawn to this one because I think there's a real gap in accessible, peer-driven finance content for people who are just starting out. The challenge I could already see was scope, since finance is a big and sensitive topic, and getting the community features right without it becoming overwhelming felt risky.

The second was a food community focused on deals and discount apps, like EatClub and TooGoodToGo. The idea was a place where people share real-time finds and help each other save money on food. I liked the practical utility of it but I wasn't convinced it was original enough. The space feels saturated and I wasn't sure what would make this hub genuinely different from just a group chat.

The third was a design community hub, a place for designers to share tools, resources, and industry insights. This one came from a real frustration I have with how scattered and noisy design content is across the internet. But if I'm honest, the concept felt a bit too generic. I couldn't see a core feature that would make it feel like something you'd actually come back to.

At this point I hadn't landed on anything I was fully convinced by. The financial literacy idea felt the most meaningful to me personally, but I was aware that what feels meaningful to design doesn't always make for the strongest technical or community case. I went into our team discussion open to being convinced otherwise.
